Electrostatics — Gauss's Law
10 marksDerivation★ Most Repeated
State and prove Gauss's law in electrostatics. Obtain the differential form. Derive Poisson's equation.
+

Statement: The total electric flux through any closed surface is equal to the total charge enclosed divided by ε₀.

Gauss's Law (Integral Form)\( \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \dfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0} \)

Proof: Consider a point charge +q at the centre of a spherical Gaussian surface of radius r.

By Coulomb's law, \(\mathbf{E} = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\hat{r}\) is radial and uniform in magnitude over the sphere.

\(\Phi = \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = E \times 4\pi r^2 = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2} \times 4\pi r^2 = \dfrac{q}{\varepsilon_0}\)

By superposition, for multiple charges: \(\Phi = \dfrac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Why not useful for asymmetric charges? Gauss's law requires a Gaussian surface where E is constant in magnitude and parallel to dS everywhere. Asymmetric charge distributions do not produce such a surface.

Differential Form: Apply Gauss's divergence theorem to the left side:

\(\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})\, dV\)

Also, \(Q_{enc} = \int_V \rho\, dV\)

Therefore: \(\int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E})\, dV = \int_V \dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\, dV\)

Differential Form\( \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \)

Poisson's Equation: Since \(\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V\):

\(\nabla \cdot (-\nabla V) = \dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Poisson's Equation\(\nabla^2 V = -\dfrac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}\)

When ρ = 0 (charge-free region): \(\nabla^2 V = 0\) — Laplace's equation.

2 marksConceptual
Gauss's law is not useful for an asymmetric distribution of charge. Why?
+
Gauss's law requires choosing a Gaussian surface over which the electric field E is uniform in magnitude and always parallel (or perpendicular) to the surface area element dS. For asymmetric charge distributions, no such surface can be constructed because E varies in both magnitude and direction. Hence the integral ∮E·dS cannot be evaluated analytically, making Gauss's law inapplicable for calculation (though it remains valid as a law).
10 marksDerivation★ Most Repeated
Apply Gauss's law to find E due to a uniformly charged sphere at points outside, on the surface, and inside. Represent graphically.
+

Consider a uniformly charged sphere of radius R, total charge Q, charge density ρ = 3Q/(4πR³).

Case 1 — Outside (r > R):

Gaussian surface: sphere of radius r (concentric). Qenc = Q.

By symmetry, E is radial and uniform: \(\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = E \cdot 4\pi r^2 = \dfrac{Q}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Outside\(E = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\)   (same as point charge)

Case 2 — On the surface (r = R):

On Surface\(E = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^2} = \dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Case 3 — Inside (r < R):

Conducting sphere: All charge resides on the surface. Qenc = 0, so E = 0 inside.

Non-conducting sphere with uniform ρ:

Qenc = ρ × (4/3)πr³ = Q(r/R)³

\(E \cdot 4\pi r^2 = \dfrac{Q r^3}{\varepsilon_0 R^3}\)

Inside (Non-conducting)\(E = \dfrac{Qr}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 R^3} = \dfrac{\rho r}{3\varepsilon_0}\)   (linear in r)

Graph: E increases linearly from 0 to a peak at r = R (the surface), then decreases as 1/r² for r > R.

[Graph] E vs r: linear rise 0→R, sharp peak at r=R, then E ∝ 1/r² beyond R
10 marksDerivation
Derive expressions for electric field at a point due to (i) an infinite line of charge and (ii) an infinite plane sheet of charge.
+

(i) Infinite Line Charge (linear charge density λ):

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length L, coaxial with the line charge.

Qenc = λL. By symmetry, E is radial and uniform over the curved surface; flux through end caps = 0.

\(\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = E \cdot 2\pi r L = \dfrac{\lambda L}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Infinite Line Charge\(E = \dfrac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\)   (E ∝ 1/r)

(ii) Infinite Plane Sheet (surface charge density σ):

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface (pillbox) of area A straddling the sheet.

Qenc = σA. By symmetry, E is perpendicular to the sheet, so flux through curved surface = 0.

\(\oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = 2EA = \dfrac{\sigma A}{\varepsilon_0}\)

Infinite Plane Sheet\(E = \dfrac{\sigma}{2\varepsilon_0}\)   (uniform, independent of distance)
Electrostatics — Electric Potential
10 marksDerivation
Obtain the relation between electric field E and electric potential V. Derive electric potential as the line integral of E.
+

Relation E and V: The work done by E in moving a unit positive charge from A to B is the potential difference VA − VB.

Work done against E over displacement dl: \(dW = -\mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}\)

Since \(dW = dV\) (change in potential energy per unit charge):

\(dV = -\mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}\)

Hence: \(\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V\)   (gradient relation)

Key Relations\(V_A - V_B = \int_B^A \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}\)  and  \(\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V\)

Potential due to a point charge:

\(E = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\). Taking V = 0 at infinity:

\(V = -\int_\infty^r E\, dr = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\)

Potential due to Point Charge\(V = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r}\)

Justification (E is vector, V is scalar): E = −∇V means the electric field is the negative gradient of V. Although V is a scalar, its directional rate of change gives the vector field E. The direction of E is always from higher to lower potential, perpendicular to equipotential surfaces.

2 marksConceptual
Earth is an equipotential surface. Explain.
+
The Earth is a large conducting body. In a conductor, charges can move freely until electrostatic equilibrium is reached, at which point there is no electric field tangential to the surface. Since E = −∇V, a zero tangential field means V does not change along the surface — making the entire surface an equipotential. Any work done in moving a charge along the Earth's surface is zero, confirming it is an equipotential.
2 marksConceptual
A point in space can have a non-zero potential even when the electric field is zero. Explain.
+
Electric field E = −∇V depends on the gradient (spatial rate of change) of potential, not its absolute value. If the potential is uniform but non-zero everywhere in a region, the gradient is zero and hence E = 0, even though V ≠ 0. Example: inside a charged conducting sphere, E = 0 but V = Q/(4πε₀R) ≠ 0.
Electrostatics — Electric Dipole
10 marksDerivation★ Very Repeated
Obtain expressions for electric potential and electric field at any point due to an electric dipole. Give results for axial and equatorial points.
+

An electric dipole consists of charges +q and −q separated by distance 2l. Dipole moment \(\mathbf{p} = q(2l)\hat{p}\). Point P is at distance r from midpoint O, at angle θ to the axis.

Electric Potential at P:

\(V = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\left(\dfrac{1}{r_1} - \dfrac{1}{r_2}\right)\) where r₁, r₂ are distances from +q and −q.

For r ≫ l: \(r_1 \approx r - l\cos\theta,\quad r_2 \approx r + l\cos\theta\)

\(V = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \dfrac{(r_2 - r_1)}{r_1 r_2} \approx \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \dfrac{2l\cos\theta}{r^2}\)

Potential at General Point\(V = \dfrac{p\cos\theta}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\)

Electric Field Components:

Radial: \(E_r = -\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial r} = \dfrac{2p\cos\theta}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\)

Tangential: \(E_\theta = -\dfrac{1}{r}\dfrac{\partial V}{\partial \theta} = \dfrac{p\sin\theta}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\)

Resultant: \(E = \sqrt{E_r^2 + E_\theta^2} = \dfrac{p}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\sqrt{1 + 3\cos^2\theta}\)

E at General Point\(E = \dfrac{p}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\sqrt{1+3\cos^2\theta}\)

Direction of E makes angle α with the radius vector where \(\tan\alpha = \dfrac{\tan\theta}{2}\).

Axial Point (θ = 0°)

\(V_{ax} = \dfrac{p}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^2}\),   \(E_{ax} = \dfrac{2p}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\) (along p)

Equatorial Point (θ = 90°)

\(V_{eq} = 0\),   \(E_{eq} = \dfrac{p}{4\pi\varepsilon_0 r^3}\) (opposite to p)

Note: \(E_{ax} = 2E_{eq}\) at the same distance r.

Electrostatics — Capacitors & Dielectrics
10 marksDerivation
Obtain expression for capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor: (i) fully filled with dielectric and (ii) partially filled with dielectric of thickness t.
+

Without dielectric:

E₀ = σ/ε₀,   V₀ = E₀d = σd/ε₀,   C₀ = Q/V₀ = ε₀A/d

(i) Fully filled with dielectric (dielectric constant κ = εᵣ):

E = E₀/κ = σ/(κε₀). The field is reduced due to polarisation opposing E₀.

V = Ed = σd/(κε₀) = Q d/(κε₀A)

C = Q/V = κε₀A/d

Capacitor Fully Filled\(C = \kappa\varepsilon_0 A/d = \varepsilon_r \varepsilon_0 A/d\)

(ii) Partially filled — dielectric slab of thickness t:

The gap can be treated as two capacitors in series: one air gap (d − t) and one dielectric (t).

V = E₀(d − t) + (E₀/κ)t = (σ/ε₀)[d − t(1 − 1/κ)]

Capacitor Partially Filled\(C = \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d - t\left(1 - \dfrac{1}{\kappa}\right)}\)

Why introducing dielectric increases C: The polarised dielectric creates a field opposing E₀, reducing the total field and hence the voltage V for the same charge Q. Since C = Q/V, capacitance increases.

10 marksDerivation
Derive expressions for energy stored per unit volume in an electric field (electric energy density).
+

Energy stored in a capacitor:

Work done to charge capacitor from 0 to Q:

\(W = \int_0^Q V\, dq = \int_0^Q \dfrac{q}{C}\, dq = \dfrac{Q^2}{2C} = \dfrac{1}{2}CV^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}QV\)

Energy density:

For a parallel plate capacitor: C = ε₀A/d, V = Ed, Volume = Ad.

\(U = \dfrac{1}{2}CV^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \cdot (Ed)^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 (Ad)\)

Energy density u = U / Volume = U/(Ad)

Electric Energy Density\(u_E = \dfrac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 = \dfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{D}\)
Magnetostatics — Biot-Savart's Law
10 marksDerivation★ Compulsory 2024 & 2025
State Biot-Savart's law. Applying it, obtain an expression for the magnetic field at any point on the axis of a circular current-carrying coil. Give the graphical representation of B vs axial distance.
+

Biot-Savart's Law: The magnetic field dB produced at a point P by a small current element I dl is:

Biot-Savart\(d\mathbf{B} = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\cdot\dfrac{I\, d\mathbf{l} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}\)

Direction: perpendicular to both dl and r̂ (right-hand rule). Magnitude: dB ∝ I dl sinφ / r² where φ is the angle between dl and r.

B at a point on the axis of a circular coil:

Setup: Circular coil of radius a, current I. Point P on the axis at distance x from centre.

For any element dl, the distance to P is \(r = \sqrt{a^2 + x^2}\). Since dl ⊥ r: dB = μ₀I dl / (4π(a²+x²)).

dB has two components: dB_x along the axis and dB_⊥ perpendicular to it.

Perpendicular components from diametrically opposite elements cancel by symmetry.

Axial component: \(dB_x = dB \cdot \sin\alpha = dB \cdot \dfrac{a}{\sqrt{a^2+x^2}}\)

Integrating around the full loop (∮ dl = 2πa):

\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \cdot \dfrac{a}{(a^2+x^2)^{3/2}} \cdot 2\pi a\)

B on Axis of Circular Coil (N turns)\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 N I a^2}{2(a^2 + x^2)^{3/2}}\)
At Centre (x = 0)

\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 NI}{2a}\)

Far from coil (x ≫ a)

\(B \approx \dfrac{\mu_0 NI a^2}{2x^3}\) (∝ 1/x³)

Graph: B is maximum at the centre, remains relatively flat near the centre, then falls steeply as ∝ 1/x³ for large x on both sides.

10 marksDerivation
State Biot-Savart's law. Obtain an expression for the magnetic field at a point due to a long straight current-carrying conductor. Also give the limiting cases.
+

Consider a long straight conductor carrying current I. Let P be a point at perpendicular distance a from the conductor.

For a small element dl at angle φ from P: \(dB = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \cdot \dfrac{dl\sin\phi}{r^2}\)

Substituting l = a cotθ, r = a/sinθ, dl = −a dθ/sin²θ:

\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi a}\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2} \sin\theta\, d\theta = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi a}(\cos\theta_1 - \cos\theta_2)\)

General Expression\(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi a}(\cos\theta_1 - \cos\theta_2)\)
Infinitely long (both ends)

θ₁ = 0°, θ₂ = 180°: \(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi a}\)

Semi-infinite (one end)

θ₁ = 0°, θ₂ = 90°: \(B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi a}\)

Direction: perpendicular to the plane containing the conductor and P (right-hand rule for solenoid).

10 marksDerivation★ Compulsory 2024 & 2025
Show that magnetic monopoles do not exist by proving div B = 0. Also show that curl B = μ₀J using Ampere's circuital law.
+

Part 1 — div B = 0 (No magnetic monopoles):

Magnetic field lines are always closed loops — they have no beginning or end (unlike electric field lines which begin and end on charges). There are no isolated magnetic charges (monopoles).

For any closed surface S: the number of B-field lines entering = the number leaving.

Therefore: \(\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0\)

Applying Gauss's divergence theorem: \(\int_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B})\, dV = 0\) for any volume V.

Gauss's Law for Magnetism\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\)  (no magnetic monopoles)

Part 2 — curl B = μ₀J (from Ampere's Circuital Law):

Ampere's Circuital Law (integral form): \(\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc}\)

Express Ienc as a surface integral of current density J: \(I_{enc} = \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{S}\)

Apply Stokes' theorem to the left side: \(\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}\)

Equating: \(\int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{B}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \mu_0 \int_S \mathbf{J} \cdot d\mathbf{S}\)

Since this holds for any surface S:

Ampere's Law (Differential Form)\(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J}\)

Note: This is Ampere's law valid for static fields only. Maxwell later showed this is incomplete (lacks the displacement current term for time-varying fields).

Magnetic Materials
10 marksExplain★ Repeated 2023, 2025
Explain the various stages of the hysteresis curve for a ferromagnetic material. Define: intensity of magnetisation, susceptibility, induction, permeability. Establish B = μ₀(H + M) and μᵣ = 1 + χₘ.
+

Hysteresis Curve (B-H Loop):

O → A: Starting from demagnetised state, H increased. B increases (magnetic domains align with H). At A, saturation is reached — all domains aligned.

A → B: H reduced to zero. B does not return to zero. At B, B = Bᵣ — the residual magnetism or retentivity. The material has become a permanent magnet.

B → C: H reversed. At C, B = 0. The reverse field needed is HC — the coercive force (coercivity). The material has been demagnetised.

C → D: H increased in reverse direction. Reverse saturation at D.

D → E → F: H brought back to zero (E: B = −Bᵣ), then increased forward (F: B = 0), back to A — completing the loop.

Hysteresis Loss: Energy dissipated per cycle = area of the B-H loop (in J/m³/cycle).

Key Definitions:

  • Intensity of Magnetisation M = magnetic moment per unit volume (A/m)
  • Magnetic susceptibility χₘ = M/H (dimensionless; positive for para/ferro, negative for dia)
  • Magnetic induction B = total flux density in material (T)
  • Magnetic permeability μ = μ₀μᵣ; ratio B/H in the material
  • Relative permeability μᵣ = μ/μ₀ (dimensionless)

Derivation of B = μ₀(H + M):

Total B inside a material has two contributions: (1) from the applied H field, and (2) from the magnetisation M of the material.

B = μ₀H + μ₀M

Fundamental Relation\(\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M})\)

Derivation of μᵣ = 1 + χₘ:

Since M = χₘH:   B = μ₀(H + χₘH) = μ₀H(1 + χₘ)

Also B = μH = μ₀μᵣH

Relation\(\mu_r = 1 + \chi_m\)

Soft iron (small hysteresis loop, low coercivity) → transformer cores. Hard steel (large loop, high retentivity) → permanent magnets.

Electromagnetic Induction
10 marksDerivation★ Repeated 5+ times
State and explain Faraday's laws of electromagnetic induction. Deduce the universal form: ∇ × E = −∂B/∂t.
+

Faraday's First Law: Whenever the magnetic flux linked with a circuit changes, an electromotive force (EMF) is induced in the circuit.

Faraday's Second Law: The magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

Faraday's Law (Lenz's Law form)\(\mathcal{E} = -\dfrac{d\Phi}{dt}\)

The negative sign (Lenz's law) indicates the induced EMF opposes the change that produced it.

Universal (Differential) Form:

EMF around a closed loop C: \(\mathcal{E} = \oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l}\)

Flux: \(\Phi = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S}\)

Faraday's law: \(\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\dfrac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = -\int_S \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\mathbf{S}\) (for fixed loop)

Apply Stokes' theorem to the left side: \(\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) \cdot d\mathbf{S}\)

Equating the two surface integrals (for any surface S):

Universal Form — Maxwell's 3rd Equation\(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)

This means: a time-varying magnetic field always generates a circulating electric field, even in empty space.

Self & Mutual Inductance
10 marksDerivation
Explain self-induction and define its coefficient. Deduce the self-inductance of a solenoid. State the factors it depends on.
+

Self-Induction: When the current through a coil changes, the changing magnetic flux through the coil induces an EMF in the same coil. This is self-induction.

Coefficient of Self-Inductance L: L = NΦ/I, or equivalently \(\mathcal{E} = -L\dfrac{dI}{dt}\). Units: Henry (H) = Wb/A = V·s/A.

Self-inductance of a solenoid (length l, N turns, cross-section area A, medium μᵣ):

Magnetic field inside: \(B = \mu_0\mu_r nI\) where n = N/l (turns per unit length)

Flux through one turn: \(\Phi = BA = \mu_0\mu_r nIA\)

Total flux linkage: \(N\Phi = N \times \mu_0\mu_r nIA = \mu_0\mu_r n^2 lA \times I\) (since N = nl)

Self-inductance: \(L = N\Phi/I = \mu_0\mu_r n^2 lA\)

Self-Inductance of Solenoid\(L = \dfrac{\mu_0 \mu_r N^2 A}{l}\)

Factors: L ∝ N² (turns), A (cross-section), μᵣ (core permeability), and 1/l (inversely with length).

Energy stored: \(U = \dfrac{1}{2}LI^2\). Magnetic energy density: \(u_B = \dfrac{B^2}{2\mu_0} = \dfrac{\mu_0 H^2}{2}\)

10 marksDerivation
Explain mutual induction and define its coefficient. Deduce the mutual inductance of two co-axial solenoids.
+

Mutual Induction: When the current in one coil (primary) changes, the varying magnetic flux induces an EMF in a nearby coil (secondary). This phenomenon is mutual induction.

Coefficient of Mutual Inductance M: \(M = N_2\Phi_{21}/I_1\), or \(\mathcal{E}_2 = -M\dfrac{dI_1}{dt}\). Units: Henry.

Mutual inductance of two co-axial solenoids (length l, same area A; primary n₁ turns/m, secondary n₂ turns/m):

B due to primary current I₁: \(B_1 = \mu_0 n_1 I_1\)

Flux through each secondary turn: \(\Phi_{21} = B_1 A = \mu_0 n_1 I_1 A\)

Total flux linkage in secondary (N₂ = n₂l turns):

\(N_2 \Phi_{21} = n_2 l \cdot \mu_0 n_1 I_1 A = \mu_0 n_1 n_2 l A \cdot I_1\)

Mutual Inductance of Coaxial Solenoids\(M = \mu_0 n_1 n_2 lA = \dfrac{\mu_0 N_1 N_2 A}{l}\)

Note: M₁₂ = M₂₁ (reciprocity theorem). Also, M = k√(L₁L₂) where k ≤ 1 is the coupling coefficient.

Maxwell's Equations — Displacement Current
10 marksDerivation★ Compulsory 2024; 7+ appearances
Show that curl H = J is inconsistent with charge conservation. How did Maxwell fix Ampere's equation? State characteristics of displacement current and derive the equation of continuity.
+

The Inconsistency:

Consider a capacitor being charged by current I. Apply Ampere's law: \(\oint \mathbf{H} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = I_{enc}\)

For surface S₁ (flat, between the plates): No conduction current passes through → ∮H·dl = 0.

For surface S₂ (curved, around the wire): I passes through → ∮H·dl = μ₀I.

Same loop gives two different results — a contradiction!

Mathematically: Taking divergence of ∇×H = J gives ∇·J = 0, implying no charge accumulation — which violates conservation of charge (∇·J = −∂ρ/∂t ≠ 0 in general).

Maxwell's Fix — Displacement Current:

Between the capacitor plates, while there is no conduction current, the electric field E changes. From Gauss's law D = ε₀E and ρ = ∇·D:

\(\dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\)

Maxwell postulated a displacement current density: \(\mathbf{J}_d = \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} = \varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\)

The displacement current Id between capacitor plates equals I (the conduction current in the wire), maintaining continuity.

Modified Ampere-Maxwell Law\(\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\)

Characteristics of Displacement Current:

  • Exists wherever the electric field changes with time (even in vacuum)
  • Produces the same magnetic effect as conduction current
  • Ensures continuity of total current across any cross-section
  • Flows between capacitor plates during charging/discharging
  • Is responsible for the propagation of electromagnetic waves

Equation of Continuity:

From Gauss's law: ∇·D = ρ, so \(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D}) = \dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\)

Take divergence of modified Ampere's law: \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{H}) = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \nabla \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} = 0\) (div of curl = 0)

Therefore: \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0\)

Equation of Continuity\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0\)

This expresses conservation of electric charge: any outflow of charge from a volume decreases the charge density inside.

Maxwell's Equations
10 marksExplain★ Compulsory 2024 & 2025
Write Maxwell's electromagnetic equations in (a) free space and (b) a dielectric medium. State five significant points of Maxwell's equations.
+
#NameFree Space (ρ=0, J=0)Dielectric Medium
1Gauss's law (Electric)\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 0\)\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{D} = \rho_f\)
2Gauss's law (Magnetic)\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\)\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\)
3Faraday's law\(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)\(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)
4Ampere-Maxwell law\(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\)\(\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J}_f + \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\)

Where in dielectric: D = εE = ε₀εᵣE, B = μH = μ₀μᵣH.

Five Significant Points:

  1. Unification: Maxwell unified all electric and magnetic phenomena into four compact equations.
  2. EM Waves Predicted: These equations predict the existence of electromagnetic waves — a purely theoretical prediction later confirmed by Hertz.
  3. Light is EM: The predicted wave speed \(c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0} = 3 \times 10^8\) m/s matches the speed of light, proving light is electromagnetic.
  4. Displacement Current: Introduced the concept of displacement current, extending Ampere's law to time-varying fields and ensuring charge conservation.
  5. Symmetry: They show a beautiful symmetry — a changing E creates B (equation 4), and a changing B creates E (equation 3), allowing self-sustaining wave propagation in vacuum.
EM Wave Equation
10 marksDerivation★ Compulsory 2024 & 2025
Deduce the electromagnetic wave equation in free space / dielectric medium. Hence show the velocity of light and that EM radiation is transverse in nature.
+

In Free Space (ρ = 0, J = 0):

Maxwell's equations give: \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\) ...(1)    \(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\) ...(2)

Take curl of equation (1): \(\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}(\nabla \times \mathbf{B})\)

Using vector identity: \(\nabla \times (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2 \mathbf{E}\)

Since ∇·E = 0 in free space, and substituting (2):

\(-\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = -\mu_0\varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}\)

Wave Equation for E\(\nabla^2 \mathbf{E} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}\)    Similarly:  \(\nabla^2 \mathbf{B} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\dfrac{\partial^2 \mathbf{B}}{\partial t^2}\)

Comparing with the standard wave equation \(\nabla^2 f = \dfrac{1}{v^2}\dfrac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}\):

Speed of Light\(c = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \times 8.854 \times 10^{-12}}} = 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}\)

In Dielectric Medium (μᵣ, εᵣ):

Replace μ₀ → μ = μ₀μᵣ and ε₀ → ε = ε₀εᵣ:

\(v = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\varepsilon}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0\mu_r\varepsilon_0\varepsilon_r}} = \dfrac{c}{\sqrt{\mu_r\varepsilon_r}}\)

Wave Velocity in Dielectric\(v = \dfrac{c}{\sqrt{\mu_r \varepsilon_r}}\)    (Refractive index \(n = \sqrt{\mu_r\varepsilon_r}\))

Transverse nature: For a plane wave propagating in the +z direction: E = E₀ cos(ωt − kz) x̂. Taking ∇·E = 0: ∂E_x/∂x = 0 (E has no component along propagation direction). Similarly ∇·B = 0 shows B is transverse. From ∇×E = −∂B/∂t: B is perpendicular to both k̂ and E. Therefore E, B, and the propagation direction k̂ are mutually perpendicular — EM waves are transverse.

Energy density equality: For a plane EM wave: \(u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2\) and \(u_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0}B^2\). Since E = cB and c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀): \(u_B = \frac{E^2}{2\mu_0 c^2} = \frac{\varepsilon_0 E^2}{2} = u_E\). Hence the ratio uE/uB = 1.

Poynting Theorem
10 marksDerivation★ 8 appearances — Most Consistent Topic
State Poynting's theorem. Obtain an expression for the Poynting vector. Explain its physical significance.
+

Poynting Vector S: The energy flux density (power per unit area) carried by an EM field. It points in the direction of energy flow.

Poynting Vector\(\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H} = \dfrac{\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}}{\mu_0}\)    Units: W/m²

Derivation:

From Maxwell's equations in a medium:

\(\nabla \times \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{J} + \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\) ...(1)     \(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\) ...(2)

Dot multiply (1) by E:   \(\mathbf{E} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{H}) = \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J} + \mathbf{E} \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t}\)

Dot multiply (2) by H:   \(\mathbf{H} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) = -\mathbf{H} \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\)

Use vector identity: \(\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) = \mathbf{H} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{E}) - \mathbf{E} \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{H})\)

Substituting:

\(\nabla \cdot (\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{H}) = -\mathbf{H} \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} - \mathbf{E} \cdot \dfrac{\partial \mathbf{D}}{\partial t} - \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J}\)

For linear media: \(\mathbf{H}\cdot\dfrac{\partial\mathbf{B}}{\partial t} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\dfrac{\mu H^2}{2}\right)\) and \(\mathbf{E}\cdot\dfrac{\partial\mathbf{D}}{\partial t} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\dfrac{\varepsilon E^2}{2}\right)\)

So: \(-\nabla \cdot \mathbf{S} = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial t}\left(\dfrac{\varepsilon E^2}{2} + \dfrac{\mu H^2}{2}\right) + \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J}\)

Integrate over volume V and apply divergence theorem:

Poynting's Theorem (Integral Form)\(-\oint_S \mathbf{S} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \dfrac{d}{dt}\int_V \left(\dfrac{\varepsilon E^2}{2} + \dfrac{\mu H^2}{2}\right) dV + \int_V \mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{J}\, dV\)

Physical meaning: The rate of energy flowing into a volume = rate of increase of stored EM energy inside + Ohmic power dissipated inside.

For a plane EM wave in free space:

\(S_{avg} = \dfrac{E_{max} H_{max}}{2} = \dfrac{E_{max}^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \dfrac{E_{max}^2}{2Z_0}\) where \(Z_0 = \sqrt{\mu_0/\varepsilon_0} \approx 377\,\Omega\) is the impedance of free space.

AC Circuits — LCR and Resonance
10 marksDerivation★ 2015, 2016, 2024, 2025
Using phasor diagrams, discuss the functioning of a series LCR circuit. Obtain the condition for resonance. Define Q-factor.
+

Consider a series circuit with resistance R, inductance L, and capacitance C connected to AC voltage \(v = V_m\sin(\omega t)\).

Phasor Analysis:

Current I is the reference phasor. Voltage phasors:

V_R = IR (in phase with I)  |  V_L = IX_L (leads I by 90°)  |  V_C = IX_C (lags I by 90°)

where \(X_L = \omega L\) (inductive reactance) and \(X_C = \dfrac{1}{\omega C}\) (capacitive reactance).

Net voltage phasor: \(V = \sqrt{V_R^2 + (V_L - V_C)^2} = I\sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2}\)

Impedance Z\(Z = \sqrt{R^2 + \left(\omega L - \dfrac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2}\)

Phase angle: \(\tan\phi = \dfrac{X_L - X_C}{R} = \dfrac{\omega L - 1/\omega C}{R}\)

If X_L > X_C: voltage leads current (inductive). If X_L < X_C: current leads voltage (capacitive).

Resonance Condition (X_L = X_C):

\(\omega_0 L = \dfrac{1}{\omega_0 C} \Rightarrow \omega_0 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}\)

Resonant Frequency\(f_0 = \dfrac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}\)

At resonance: Z = R (minimum); I = V/R (maximum); φ = 0 (unity power factor); V_L = V_C and may be much greater than V (voltage amplification); circuit is purely resistive.

Q-factor (quality factor): \(Q = \dfrac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \dfrac{1}{\omega_0 CR} = \dfrac{1}{R}\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{C}}\). Q = V_L/V = V_C/V at resonance (voltage magnification factor).

Series = Acceptor circuit because impedance is minimum and current is maximum at resonance, so the circuit "accepts" maximum energy at the resonant frequency.

+

Why Acceptor/Rejector?

Series circuit: At resonance, impedance is minimum and current is maximum. The circuit accepts maximum power from the source — hence acceptor.

Parallel circuit: At resonance, impedance is maximum and current drawn from supply is minimum. The circuit effectively rejects the resonant frequency from passing — hence rejector.
